## Why a gentle start matters

After childbirth, your body needs time to recover. This applies not only to the physical state, but also to the emotional one: fatigue, a new rhythm of life, and a changed sense of your own body. Trying to "get back in shape" too quickly often leads to the opposite effect.

A common misconception is that it is enough to simply "start training like before." But the postpartum body works differently: posture, muscle tone, and recovery capacity have all changed. What matters is not just starting, but how the training is structured.

## When can you start

There is no universal answer. Much depends on how the birth went, how you feel, and what your physician recommended. For some, it is 6 weeks. For others, it may be several months. You can read more about this in the article on [when to start returning to activity after childbirth](/en/blog/when-to-start-returning-to-activity-after-childbirth/).

Important: the decision to begin training is best made together with your doctor, especially if there were complications or a [C-section](/en/blog/returning-to-movement-after-c-section/).

## Where to start

### Breathing and body awareness

The first step is not load, but reconnecting with your body. Breathing practices, gentle core activation, and pelvic floor awareness form the foundation for everything that follows. This is not a "warm-up before the real workout" — it is a full and important stage of recovery.

### Pelvic floor and core attention

After pregnancy and childbirth, the pelvic floor muscles and deep core muscles need focused attention. Diastasis, posture changes, and a feeling of instability in the core all require careful work, not standard ab exercises.

### No comparisons

Every postpartum journey is individual. Comparing yourself to others or to your "previous shape" leads to frustration. What matters more is understanding what your body needs right now.

### Gradual progression

Increasing load should be smooth and based on how you feel, not on a fixed schedule. A good specialist helps build a route, not push toward a result.
